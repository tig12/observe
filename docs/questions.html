Expected values

Are control groups necessary ?

Control groups are computed to build the "expected" data that we should theoretically observe.
But the method described by Didier Castille in his article "A Link between Birth and Death" ("Un Lien entre la Naissance et le Décès") permits to compute theoretical data without control groups.
So can we perform valid tests without control groups ?

But another consideration leads to pose the opposite question: is the method without control groups appropriate for these tests ? This question comes from an observation: most distributions involving venus show a pattern of 5 occurences (see for example the distributions of venus in the "Births in France in 2000" study).
This is possibly related to the astronomical movement of venus seen from the earth (see for example here).
This is logical: due to its apparent movement, venus is more often located at certain positions. And computing the theoretical value of a cell with the formula:
"mean of the line x mean of the column / total of the table" erases the irregularity of the movement and then produces a high value for chi square.
If this is true, it means that this computation of theoretical values is not able to eliminate astronomical irregularities.

How to compute control groups ?

Inthe "Death in France" study, current code just loops on birth dates, and randomly selects another person to take his / her death date. The choice of a computation method is arbitrary, and it affects the resulting control groups, which may affect the results of statistical tests. So this question seems very important to correctly perform an analysis.
